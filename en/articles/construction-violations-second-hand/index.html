Building Violations in Resale Homes: Identification & Liability

Written by Adv. Eliram Elgarably Real Estate Attorney, practicing since 2013

When purchasing a resale apartment in Israel, one of the most common legal and planning pitfalls is the existence of building violations. These violations expose the buyer to judicial orders, heavy fines, and severe difficulties in securing a mortgage.

What is Considered a Building Violation?

A building violation is any physical change made to a property without a lawful permit or in violation of an existing permit. Examples include enclosing balconies, building interior lofts (mezzanines), adding unpermitted storage sheds in the yard, splitting an apartment into units, or altering exterior walls.

Impact of Violations on Mortgage Approval

Before issuing a mortgage, banks send an appraiser to evaluate the property. The appraiser compares the physical structure with building permits and explicitly notes any violations. Serious violations can lead to the bank refusing the mortgage or devaluing the property significantly.

Who is Liable, and How to Protect Yourself?

Once title passes, the new owner can face enforcement proceedings - including fines and demolition orders - for maintaining or using the unpermitted construction, even though the violation predates the purchase. Therefore, discovering them during due diligence is essential. If legalizable violations exist, the contract must hold the seller accountable to resolve them or keep substantial funds in escrow to secure compliance.
